Language Evolution Simulator

Ongoing  ·  Solo  ·  Genetic Algorithms  ·  Computational Linguistics

A genetic-algorithm simulation of language emergence from scratch. Agents are placed in a world with no pre-designed phonology, no vocabulary, and no grammar. They produce arbitrary sounds and are selected by how well they communicate goal-relevant information to other agents. Over thousands of generations, structure emerges.

What has emerged

The current simulation has produced a fully emergent artificial language with deterministic compositional grammar. Agents achieve 86% role-decoding accuracy across communicative scenarios. The lexicon contains approximately 50 words organised around CVC (consonant-vowel-consonant) phonological templates that emerged without any phonological bias being specified.

Four grammatical categories have appeared spontaneously: number, degree, aspect, and magnitude. Beyond these, the language has developed evidentiality marking (how the speaker knows something) and tense, both of which are cross-linguistically widespread in natural languages but were entirely absent from the simulation's initial conditions.

Design

The simulation runs in discrete generations. Each agent has a genome encoding production biases and a perceptual decoder. Fitness is evaluated on communicative success in paired tasks: one agent observes a scene and must transmit enough information for a partner to act correctly. Agents with higher success rates reproduce with higher probability; offspring inherit genomes with mutation. There is no teacher, no target language, and no reward signal specifying what grammar should look like.

The compositional structure in the resulting grammar was not designed in. It is a convergent solution: compositionality reduces the memory load required to communicate a large number of distinct situations, so it tends to be selected for when the communicative space is large enough.

Status

The simulation is ongoing. Current work focuses on scaling the scenario space to test whether the existing grammatical categories generalise or fragment under pressure from a richer communicative environment, and on characterising the transition from holistic to compositional signalling more precisely.

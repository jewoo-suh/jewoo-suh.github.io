Voynich Decode Project

Closed  ·  Solo  ·  Statistical Analysis  ·  Computational Linguistics

The Voynich Manuscript is a 15th-century illustrated codex written entirely in an unknown script. It has resisted decipherment for over a century. The leading hypotheses divide broadly into two camps: either the text encodes a natural or constructed language (the cipher hypothesis), or it is a deliberate fabrication with no semantic content.

Approach

Rather than attempting decipherment, this project approached the manuscript statistically: if the cipher hypothesis is correct, the text should behave like a language under pressure. Natural languages, and even well-designed ciphers of natural language, exhibit specific statistical signatures at the character, word, and structural levels: Zipf-like rank-frequency distributions, bounded entropy profiles, morphological regularities, and cross-section consistency.

The project reproduced the structural properties of Voynichese — its character distributions, word-length profiles, positional constraints on glyphs, and inter-word entropy — via a combinatorial generative procedure of approximately 200 units. The procedure is purely mechanical: it draws from a small inventory of glyph templates and applies a set of positional rules without reference to any semantic content.

Findings

The generated text matches the Voynich Manuscript across all tested statistical dimensions. This demonstrates that every measured property of the manuscript is consistent with a content-free generative process. It does not require a language, a cipher, or any underlying meaning.

The ridge plot above shows the comparison of Voynichese character-level distributions against a range of natural languages and constructed controls. Voynichese clusters with the synthetic controls rather than with natural languages, which supports the content-free interpretation.

The cipher hypothesis predicts structure that is not there. The project concludes that the Voynich Manuscript is most parsimoniously explained as a content-free artefact produced by a procedure like the one reconstructed here, falsifying the cipher hypothesis at the statistical level.

Status

The project is closed. The analysis reached a conclusion; further work would require either new manuscript data or a different framing of the question.

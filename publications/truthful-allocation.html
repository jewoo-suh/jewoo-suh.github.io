Truthful Task and Resource Allocation in Leader–Follower Agent Organisations

J. Suh et al.
PRIMA 2026  ·  In preparation, 2026

This paper investigates the following scenario: a leader has to hand out tasks and split a limited, productive resource among self-interested agents who privately know how competent they are. The leader is assigning tasks and resources to each subordinate commander; however, the leader has limited information and cannot either verify individual commander reports on self-confidence or use money as an incentive (as money does not exist in this scenario). Given these conditions, can the leader still get them to report truthfully, and what does it cost?

The key observation is structural. Under a natural model of how competence and resource translate into success, each agent’s expected payoff turns out to be linear in its private competence, which reduces the multi-variable screening problem to a simpler linear system. From there, the paper solves the ideal full-information allocation (matching agents to missions, then “water-filling” the budget across them), shows that naively reusing that allocation on self-reported competence is not truthful, and that the profitable lie even flips direction with how heavily the resource is weighted: agents sandbag when it is small and over-claim when it is large. It then gives the leader-optimal truthful mechanism, with a welfare loss (a “price of truthfulness”) that is only second-order in the incentive slack.

The predictions were tested behaviourally. Open-weight LLM agents in repeated play learn to exploit the naive rule exactly as the theory predicts, and learn to report truthfully under the corrected mechanism, with misreporting falling from 54% to 12%.
